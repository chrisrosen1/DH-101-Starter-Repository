# Sustainability & Ethics
## My Research
AI may feel like a simple and low-impact tool, but it depends on large systems that use significant amounts of energy. One of the biggest things I found is that training models like GPT-3 requires a lot of electricity, about 1,287 MWh, and produces around 552 tons of CO2. These numbers are hard to understand on their own, so comparing them to things like cars and flights made the impact more clear.

I also found that AI can sometimes be more efficient than humans for certain tasks, like writing or creating images. Some studies show that AI can produce much less CO2 than humans doing the same work. However, this does not mean AI has no impact. Because AI is used so often and by so many people, the total environmental cost can still be very large.

## Personal Reflection
My AI Use Habits

I use AI mostly for schoolwork, like brainstorming ideas, understanding concepts, and getting unstuck when I’m not sure how to start something. It’s something I tend to use quickly and casually without thinking much about the impact behind it.

Estimated Impact

I know that one use is small, but it adds up over time. Since I use AI regularly, my overall impact is probably larger than I would expect, even if each use feels insignificant.

What I’m Willing to Change

I can be more intentional about when I use AI. I don’t need to use it for small things that I can easily do on my own, like quick questions or things I already understand.

What I Won’t Change (and Why)

I don’t think I will stop using AI completely. It is too useful for school and helps me work more efficiently. The benefits are hard to ignore, even knowing there are environmental costs.
## Systemic vs. Individual
What Individuals Can Do

Individuals can use AI more intentionally and avoid unnecessary use. Small changes, like using it less often or only when needed, can reduce overall impact.

What Requires Systemic Change

Most of the impact comes from how AI systems are built and powered. Companies control how large models are, how often they are trained, and what kind of energy is used. These are not things individuals can change.

Who Is Responsible

Responsibility is shared, but not equally. Individuals play a role through their usage, but companies and developers have much more control over the overall environmental impact. Real change depends on both, but especially on larger systems.
