# What does it mean to be human in the age of AI?

I learned a lot about the relationship between humans and AI over the semester. At the beginning of the course, I did not really understand that relationship or think about it when I was using AI. I mostly focused on the negative aspects and saw it as something that could just do work for you.

Over the semester, each make taught me something new about how AI actually works. I started to see how useful AI can be in many different ways, especially in creative projects. At the same time, I learned that for AI to be useful, it requires human interaction. The results depend on the choices, prompts, and decisions made by the person using it.

I also began to think about parts of AI that I had never considered before. I had not thought about the resources it uses, such as data storage, energy, or the people working behind the scenes to make these systems function. As we become more dependent on AI, an important question is how we, as a society, will manage these systems and their impact.

One of the most important course readings that shaped my thinking came from On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots by Emily Bender. In this reading, Bender argues that AI systems do not actually understand language or meaning. Instead, they predict patterns based on large amounts of data. This helped me realize that even though AI outputs can sound or look realistic, they are not based on true understanding. I saw this clearly in my AI selfie project. I used prompts to describe myself and generate an image, but the result did not actually look like me. It created a more polished and generic version of a person. This showed me that AI can produce convincing results, but it does not truly understand identity. Being human in the age of AI means recognizing the difference between appearance and meaning.

Another important theme from this course is that technology is not neutral. In Hacking the Cis-tem, Marie Hicks explains how early computer systems in Britain were built around strict gender categories that excluded transgender people. These systems made it difficult for people to change their records, even when it affected their jobs, income, and daily lives. Hicks shows that this bias was not accidental—it was built into the system. This helped me understand that technology reflects the values of the people who create it. This idea connects to AI today, where systems can reproduce bias from the data they are trained on. In my own work, I saw this in the AI selfie project, where the image appeared more “perfect” than a real person. This suggests that AI reflects certain standards and assumptions. Being human in the age of AI means questioning these systems and understanding who they include and exclude.

A third important idea comes from Anatomy of an AI System by Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler. This reading shows that AI is not just software, but part of a much larger system that depends on labor, data, and natural resources. Devices like Amazon Echo may seem simple, but they rely on global supply chains, mining, and human labor such as data labeling. One idea that stood out to me is that users are not just consumers. They are also helping train AI. Every interaction with AI produces data that improves the system. This means that AI depends on human activity in ways that are often hidden. Being human in the age of AI means recognizing these systems and thinking about the people and resources behind the technology.

One of the biggest ideas I learned in this course is that creativity is about making decisions. AI can generate endless images, text, and ideas, but it does not know what is meaningful. That role still belongs to humans. In my bots and generators project, I created a system where I used one prompt and only changed the location each time. At first, I thought I was doing the assignment wrong because it was not fully automated. But I realized that my rule was the generator. The creativity came from how I designed the system and how I chose which results to keep. This showed me that AI generates options, but humans create meaning by selecting, refining, and experimenting.

I also saw the limits of AI in my GIF project. I used AI to generate repeated images, and the system was very good at reproducing the same image. However, it struggled to create variation. Even when I changed the prompts, the results often looked very similar. This showed me that AI is strong at pattern recognition and repetition, but not at true originality. Human creativity is different because it involves new ideas, personal experiences, and meaning. This reinforced the idea that AI can support creativity, but it cannot replace it.

The course also made me think about the ethical impact of AI, especially in terms of labor and inequality. In Anatomy of an AI System, Crawford and Joler explain how AI depends on hidden human labor. These workers often do repetitive and difficult tasks for low pay, while large companies make significant profits. This shows that AI is not just about technology, but also about power and inequality. At the same time, another article we read argued that AI can produce fewer carbon emissions than humans for tasks like writing and illustrating. This created an important tension. AI may be more efficient in some ways, but it also raises concerns about job loss, ethics, and long-term impact. Being human in the age of AI means understanding these tradeoffs and making thoughtful decisions about how we use technology.

Overall, this course taught me that AI is not something separate from humans—it is deeply connected to human systems, decisions, and values. Being human in the age of AI means making choices, asking questions, and staying actively involved in the process. AI can generate endless possibilities, but it does not know what matters. That responsibility still belongs to humans.
In the end, AI does not define what it means to be human. Instead, it makes human creativity, judgment, and responsibility more important than ever.
